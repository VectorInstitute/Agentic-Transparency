Abstract

Over the past decade, artificial intelligence (AI) has evolved from static predictive models to generative systems capable of interaction, reasoning, and adaptation. The latest step in this progression is Agentic AI, which builds on large language models (LLMs) by coupling their language understanding with autonomous decision-making and goal-directed behavior.

As these systems gain greater autonomy and operate in richer environments, they also become more opaque. Traditional interpretability and explainability methods, originally designed for static predictive models, are increasingly insufficient to capture the evolving internal states and decision processes of Agentic AI systems.

In this work, we present a comprehensive survey that synthesizes interpretability and explainability for Agentic AI across three stages: (1) design-time transparency, (2) process-level interpretability, and (3) outcome-level explanations. We introduce X-AXIOM, a taxonomy that organizes transparency for LLM-based agents along five axes: Cognitive Objects (WHAT), Assurance and Evaluation Objectives (WHY), Mechanisms (HOW), Temporal Stages (WHEN), and Multi-agent / socio-technical extensions (WHO).

Key Contributions

1. X-AXIOM Taxonomy

A comprehensive framework organizing transparency along five axes: WHAT, WHY, HOW, WHEN, and WHO, providing a shared vocabulary for interpretability, explainability, and governance.

2. Cognitive Audit Surface

Formalize a cognitive audit surface (intent, beliefs, plans, memory, tool I/O, policies, outcomes) with six assurance objectives: faithfulness, usefulness, compliance, robustness, equity, and auditability.

3. Comprehensive Survey

Survey methods from interpretability, explainable AI, mechanistic interpretability, and operational monitoring, mapping them into the X-AXIOM space.

4. Evaluation Protocols

Outline evaluation protocols that align design-time, process-time, and outcome-time checks with the six assurance objectives.

The Five Axes

1. Cognitive Objects (WHAT)

What internal states and interfaces should be observable: intent, beliefs, plans, memory, tool I/O, policies/guardrails, and outcomes.

2. Assurance & Evaluation Objectives (WHY)

Why transparency is needed:

  • Faithfulness: Explanations reflect actual computations
  • Usefulness: Explanations are actionable for stakeholders
  • Compliance: Evidence supports legal and ethical requirements
  • Robustness: Explanations are stable under perturbations
  • Equity: Fair treatment across groups
  • Auditability: Artifacts can be reproduced and verified

3. Mechanisms (HOW)

How to achieve transparency:

  • Intrinsic: Self-rationales, plan graphs
  • Post hoc: Attribution, counterfactuals, prototypes
  • Causal/Mechanistic: Activation patching, circuits
  • Operational: Signed logs, replay
  • Social: Dialogue, role rationales

4. Temporal Stages (WHEN)

When in the lifecycle: Design-time, Process-time, and Outcome-time transparency checks.

5. Multi-agent Add-ons (WHO)

Who is accountable in multi-agent systems: Role-rationale contracts, attribution mappings, and provenance graphs.
